Agile Fundamentals

Theory foundations for modern data‑driven product teams

0. Historical Snapshot

The ideas behind Agile reach back to Japanese lean-manufacturing: the Toyota Production System (TPS) of the 1950s–1970s introduced kanban cards, just-in-time flow, and relentless kaizen (continuous improvement). These concepts crossed over into product-development theory—most famously in Takeuchi & Nonaka’s 1986 HBR article that likened high-performing teams to a “rugby scrum.” During the 1990s internet boom, several lightweight methods—Scrum, Extreme Programming (XP), Crystal, DSDM—experimented with short iterations and customer feedback loops. In February 2001, seventeen practitioners gathered in Snowbird, Utah and distilled their shared mindset into the Agile Manifesto. Since then, Agile has expanded far beyond software, but its heartbeat remains short cycles, transparency, and adaptation.

  • 1948 – 1975 — Toyota Production System (TPS) – Taiichi Ohno pioneers kanban cards, JIT flow, and kaizen on Toyota factory floors.
  • 1986 — “New New Product Development Game” – Takeuchi & Nonaka coin the scrum metaphor, studying Toyota, Honda, Fuji-Xerox.
  • 1994 — DSDM formalises time-boxed delivery in the UK.
  • 1995 — Scrum presented by Jeff Sutherland & Ken Schwaber at OOPSLA.
  • 1999 — XP popularises Test-Driven Development and pair-programming (Kent Beck).
  • Feb 2001 — Agile Manifesto signed at Snowbird ski resort.
  • 2005 – 2010 — Kanban for Knowledge Work adapts Toyota flow principles to software and DevOps.

1. The Agile Manifesto

Four Core Values

  • Individuals & Interactions over processes & tools
  • Working Software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer Collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to Change over following a plan

Twelve Supporting Principles

  1. Satisfy the customer through early & continuous delivery.
  2. Welcome changing requirements — even late in development.
  3. Deliver working software frequently (weeks rather than months).
  4. Business & developers work together daily.
  5. Build projects around motivated individuals & trust them.
  6. Convey information face‑to‑face when possible.
  7. Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  8. Maintain a sustainable development pace.
  9. Continuous attention to technical excellence & good design.
  10. Simplicity—maximise the work not done.
  11. Self‑organising teams create best results.
  12. Reflect & tune behaviour at regular intervals.

2. Empirical Process Control

Scrum is founded on empiricism—knowledge comes from experience and evidence‑based decisions. Three pillars uphold every implementation:

Transparency

Significant aspects of the process must be visible to those responsible for the outcome. A common Definition of Done and visible backlog items achieve this.

Inspection

Frequent checks on artefacts (backlog, increments, practice) detect undesirable variances or problems.

Adaptation

Adjust the process or product quickly when a deviation is detected. Sprint Retrospectives and Reviews enable this pillar.

3. Scrum Framework — Roles, Events & Artefacts

3.1 Roles

Product Owner

Maximises product value & manages the Product Backlog.

Scrum Master

Facilitator ensuring Scrum is understood & enacted.

Developers

Cross‑functional professionals creating each Increment.

3.2 Events

Sprint

A time‑boxed container (usually ≤ 1 month, for us is just 1 week).

Planning

Set Sprint Goal & plan work.

Daily Scrum

15‑min inspect/adapt.

Review

Show Increment & update backlog.

Retrospective

Improve process & team.

3.3 Artefacts

Product Backlog

Ordered list of everything needed for the product.

Sprint Backlog

Plan for delivering selected items & achieving Sprint Goal.

Increment

Usable, potentially releasable work meeting Definition of Done.

4. Definition of Done vs. Acceptance Criteria

Definition of Done (DoD) is a shared, universal checklist that every backlog item must satisfy to be considered complete. Acceptance Criteria are item‑specific conditions that must be met for that item to be accepted by the Product Owner.

  • DoD example: Code reviewed & merged, tests pass, documentation updated, deployed to staging.
  • Acceptance Criteria example: Forecast API returns JSON within 200 ms.

6. Agile Metrics & Visualisations

Good metrics make agility visible and evidence‑based. For postgraduate learners, the link to statistical thinking is direct: you’ll forecast delivery the same way you forecast markets.

  • Velocity: Average story‑points completed per sprint; useful for capacity planning.
  • Burndown / Burn‑up charts: Track how work trends toward done over the sprint or release; spot scope‑creep.
  • Cumulative‑flow diagram (CFD): Shows work‑item ageing and bottlenecks across workflow states; interpretable via Little’s Law.
  • Lead & Cycle Time: Statistical distribution of time from idea to release; lower variance => predictable delivery.

7. Estimation Techniques

Agile estimation expresses uncertainty rather than precision. Choose the lightest method that informs prioritisation:

  1. Planning Poker: Team silently chooses Fibonacci‑style points, then discusses variance. Encourages consensus and exposes hidden work.
  2. T‑shirt Sizing: XS – XL labels give rough relative effort fast; perfect for grooming large backlogs.
  3. Three‑point (PERT): (optimistic + 4×most likely + pessimistic)/6. Bridges Agile with statistical risk—ideal for research spikes where variance matters.

9. XP Engineering Practices

Extreme Programming (XP) complements Scrum with technical excellence—the foundation of sustainable speed.

  • Test‑Driven Development (TDD): Write failing test → code → refactor. Creates live specification and regression safety‑net.
  • Pair‑Programming: Two brains, one keyboard; improves code quality and team knowledge diffusion.
  • Continuous Integration: Every commit built & tested automatically. Shrinks feedback loop and integrates data science pipelines.

10. Scaling Frameworks (Enterprise Glance)

When multiple teams collaborate on a single product, lightweight scaling frameworks align cadence and architecture:

  • SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) — Program Increments & Agile Release Trains.
  • LeSS (Large‑Scale Scrum) — One Product Backlog, 1–8 Scrum teams sharing events.
  • Nexus — Extension to Scrum adding integration‑team & Nexus Sprint Backlog.

Tip: First master one‑team Scrum; scale only if coordination cost outweighs overhead.

11. Agile Anti‑patterns & Smells

Recognising failure modes early saves projects:

  • Zombie Scrum: Ceremonies happen but no Increment of value is shipped.
  • Mini‑Waterfalls: Analysis → dev → test inside a sprint causes hidden queues.
  • Scope Creep masked as change: Adds work with no Sprint Goal realignment.
  • Velocity Misuse: Used as performance KPI → gaming & false reporting.

12. Psychological Safety & Team Dynamics

Google’s Project Aristotle found psychological safety to be the number‑one predictor of high‑performing teams. For Agile, this means:

  • Members feel safe to speak up about impediments and ideas without blame.
  • Retrospectives surface hard truths, enabling genuine adaptation.
  • Innovation thrives when “failure” is reframed as rapid feedback.
